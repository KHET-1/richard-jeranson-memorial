Things you can do from the kitchen table to fill in his story before May 20, with phone numbers you can actually call and websites that are free. Most of these will get you something useful in under 30 minutes if you have the patience to dial a phone.

His Naval service records

He was Class V‑6 USNR Petty Officer 3rd Class, honorable discharge from NAS Corpus Christi 1946, called back for the Korean War. Every paper trail of that service exists somewhere — here’s where to ask.

  • National Personnel Records Center (NPRC) — St. Louis, MO
    Free for next-of-kin (his children, or grandchildren if his children are deceased). Submit Form SF‑180 and you’ll get his full DD‑214 and service jacket in 6–12 weeks. archives.gov/veterans/military-service-records
  • U.S. Navy Memorial — Navy Log search
    Free name-search of veteran records. Search “Richard Jeranson” and any spelling variants. navylog.navymemorial.org/search-navy-log
  • Fold3 — US WWII Navy Muster Rolls 1938–1949
    These are the quarterly lists at NAS Corpus Christi 1944–1946. Will show his exact unit, every shipmate, every assignment. Free if you sign in at most Minnesota public libraries with a library card. fold3.com — WWII Navy Muster Rolls
  • Minnesota Military Museum — Veterans Registry
    Search by name. Free. mnmilitarymuseum.org/veterans/registry
  • Minnesota Korean War Bonus Index at the Minnesota Historical Society — if he claimed his state Korean War bonus payment, he’s in this index. libguides.mnhs.org/milservice/kvw
  • Duluth Public Library — War Casualties Index
    Indexes Duluth News-Tribune articles on WWII, Korean, and Vietnam War servicemen from the Duluth area — including enlistment notices and homecomings. 218‑730‑4200 · duluthlibrary.org — local history

His patents

He held several patents. The family remembers a hand-pedaled bicycle and guitar modifications. Once you have the exact spelling of the name on the patent (could be “Richard C. Jeranson,” could be his birth surname, could be the shop’s business name as assignee), these databases will surface every one of them.

  • Google Patents — free, comprehensive, image of every patent page. patents.google.com
  • USPTO Patent Public Search — the official US database, free. ppubs.uspto.gov
  • Free Patents Online — alternative front-end if Google Patents misses anything. freepatentsonline.com

Tip: if you find one of his patents, check the “assignee” field. That’s the name of the company or shop the patent was filed under, and it will tell you the exact legal name of his pattern shop — which itself unlocks Minnesota Secretary of State business filings.

The records below will help fill in dates, places, and missing names — especially for Joni, Judy, Gary, Rick, and Bernice/June (each will have their own thread to pull on).

  • 1940 Census — he would be age 12 in Ernst’s household. Will show Ernst’s wife (his adoptive mother), siblings if any, the family address, occupations. Free with a FamilySearch login. familysearch.org/1940census
  • 1950 Census — he would be 22, just back from his Navy discharge. Released to the public in April 2022. Free with FamilySearch. familysearch.org/1950census
  • Minnesota Adoption Research — FamilySearch wiki page covering Minnesota’s pre‑1977 adoption record access (his adoption likely happened in the 1930s). familysearch.org — Minnesota adoption
  • Minnesota Historical Society — Birth Records · 651‑259‑6800 · mnhs.org/people/birthrecords
  • St. Louis County Courthouse — Duluth · Birth, death, and marriage records from 1870 forward, including 1927. stlouiscountymn.gov — genealogy requests
  • Find A Grave — search Bernice Blaske and June Parks here. Many Minnesota cemetery records are indexed with photos, dates, and family links. findagrave.com
  • Legacy.com Minnesota obituaries — search Bernice Blaske and June Parks for their obituaries, which usually list surviving family at the time and can connect them directly to the Jeranson line. legacy.com — Minnesota

One thing the family already gave us

A different Dick Jeranson is buried at Fort Snelling already — an Army WWII veteran (D‑Day + 1, France to Germany, Bronze Star and three Purple Hearts) who died in 2002. Different person, but the surname rarity makes him almost certainly family — possibly an older brother of your grandpa or another adopted son in Ernst’s line.

Also already at Fort Snelling: Richard Ernest Jeranson (1954–2010, U.S. Navy, Section 27 Site 2124). The middle name Ernest carries the Ernst-Jeranson family thread through the next generation. Mom may already know exactly who these two are.

If she does, write that down — it’s the kind of detail that gets lost when the people who know it pass on.

What to keep an eye out for

  • His birth surname before Ernst adopted him.
  • His patent numbers (start with “US” followed by 7 digits).
  • His shop’s legal business name.
  • The full names of his adoptive mother (Ernst’s wife) and any siblings.
  • Bernice Blaske’s and June Parks’ full names — maiden names, married names, hometowns, and how exactly they were related to him.
  • His service number from the Navy — usually printed on his discharge papers.
  • The name of his ship or unit at NAS Corpus Christi.
  • Photographs of him in uniform, with his guitar, or in the shop.

Even one of these unlocks several others. Don’t worry about getting them all at once.
